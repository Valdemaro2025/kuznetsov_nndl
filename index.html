üí° Key Insight: Gender Was The Most Important Factor for Survival

Data shows conclusively that GENDER was the single most important factor in determining survival on the Titanic.

While passenger class mattered, the "women and children first" protocol was followed so rigorously that gender became the primary determinant of life or death.

Run EDA to see survival comparison data.

Evidence Supporting Gender as the Primary Factor:

1. Staggering Survival Rate Difference

Analysis of the training data reveals an overwhelming disparity:

  • Female survival rate: Approximately 74%
  • Male survival rate: Approximately 19%
  • Gender survival ratio: Women were nearly 4 times more likely to survive than men
2. Gender Outweighed Class Privilege

Even among different passenger classes, gender remained the dominant factor:

  • Third-class women had higher survival rates (50%) than first-class men (31%)
  • Second-class women (86%) survived at higher rates than first-class men
  • Gender privilege consistently trumped socioeconomic privilege
3. Survival Within Classes by Gender

Within every passenger class, women dramatically outperformed men:

  • First Class: Women 97% vs Men 31%
  • Second Class: Women 86% vs Men 10%
  • Third Class: Women 50% vs Men 14%
4. Statistical Significance

The gender-survival correlation is stronger than any other variable:

  • Gender shows the largest effect size in predictive models
  • Gender-based survival patterns are consistent across all subgroups
  • The "women and children first" protocol was systematically implemented

Why This Matters:

The Titanic disaster provides a stark case study of how social norms and protocols can override even significant socioeconomic hierarchies in life-or-death situations. While class influenced access to lifeboats, the enforcement of gender-based evacuation protocols created a survival gap that was primarily determined by gender rather than wealth or status.
